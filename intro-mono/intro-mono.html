Intro Mono

Intro Mono

Intro's playful cousing. Playing off the inherently chaotic patterns of monospaced characters I felt it was appropriate to add a bit more idiosyncracy to a few of the characters, namely /S, /g, and /y. In an effort to subtly distinguish it from it's workhorse cousin, I relinquished the horizontal terminals, giving Intro Mono a sharp and authoritative presence when set at large sizes. Balancing vertical inner strokes with round outer gives it a feeling of being carefully machined. Widening the joints of diagonal characters gives it an especially slight and sharp look in lighter weights, and provides a bit of visual relief as the weight increases. As is often the case with monospaced fonts, many of the letters have been stretched, some more dramatically than others, in an effort to fill the void around them. As much as possible I kept the original geometry and proportions of Intro Sans intact in an effort to keep them a cohesive family.

Intro Mono includes fractions, full Latin extended language support for 60+ languages, extended currency symbols, mathematical symbols, arrrows, and sub/superscript. Currently it is only available in roman (upright) but an italic companion is in the works.

In the course of the history of typewriters — roughly 100 years — the typefaces designed for the machine grew in number and evolved in shape. The shiftless alphabet that was used in the first commercially manufactured typewriter was soon replaced by a slab-serif design, which would become the unofficial standard among manufacturers in the first half of the 20th century. Most companies had their own Pica typeface, a monospaced font that fit ten characters to the inch. The design of the characters and the printing differences among models were used back then for typewriting identification.

This type style quickly spread, but it was not the only option available. Sans serif, script, and italic fonts were also included in early typewriter-font catalogs. There were typefaces created for particular purposes, such as protective writing that perforated paper instead of inking it. The shiftless alphabets were mainly used for advertising, telegrams, and financial work; and the italic and script faces were intended for informal documents like personal correspondence.

Monospaced typefaces were born from the limitations of mechanical typewriters. When the typist pressed a key, the carriage moved the paper the same distance each time, so it was easier to return to a previous printing point to make corrections and do tabulation work. The terms “typewriter” and “monospace” have a common genesis but are not interchangeable. We can find typefaces created for the machine with different character widths, and some monospaced typefaces designed today have no aesthetic or historical link to typewriters.
